Wait for Signal

Push button · Deep red

The red man stands motionless, hands slightly away from his sides, waiting. He has been waiting since 1969, when the first pedestrian crossing signals were installed in the UK. Fifty-seven years of standing still, teaching us patience.

On Light

This is not merely red light — it is red light in fog, in moisture, in the particular atmosphere of a February evening in Yorkshire. The LED array behind the figure creates a halo effect, a bloom that extends far beyond the boundaries of the signal housing. The red bleeds into the air itself.

The exposure captures something the eye normalises away: the sheer intensity of this red. It dominates the frame, painting the pole, the pavement, the photographer's own reflection in the glass. In the background, buildings become suggestions, memories of architecture seen through a veil of crimson.

On the Figure

Look at the posture. The red man is not aggressive, not warning — he is simply present, standing with a certain dignity. His form is deliberately androgynous, universal, reduced to the essential elements of human shape. Head, torso, arms, legs. Nothing more.

Below him, barely visible, the green walking man waits in darkness. Two states of being, two possible futures, contained in one small box.

On Instruction

"Push button. Wait for signal." The text at the bottom of the frame is almost lost in the red wash, but its presence matters. This is public space speaking to its inhabitants: here is what you must do, here is the correct behaviour.

The British pedestrian crossing is a social contract made visible. We agree to wait. We agree to trust that the green man will come. We agree that this small glowing figure has authority over our movement.

On Colour Theory

Red is the colour of stop, of warning, of attention demanded. This is not arbitrary — human eyes contain more receptors for red light than for any other colour. We evolved to notice red: the red of blood, of ripe fruit, of fire. The red man exploits millions of years of biological programming.

But red is also warmth. And in this image, shot on a cold night, there is something almost comforting about this glow. The red man is not hostile. He is simply asking you to pause, to notice, to be present in this moment at this crossing.
